System Design · Lesson 02

Consistency & Availability patterns

The dense middle of the System Design Primer — untangled, in plain English.

~12 min Skill: pick a consistency & failover model on purpose Win: you can read the "nines" and not bluff

This is the section of the Primer1 people skim and then quietly hope nobody asks about. It's actually two simple ideas wearing scary words:

  • Consistency — after I write data, when does everyone else see it?
  • Availability — when a server dies, does the system stay up? And how do we even measure "up"?
The whole lesson in one line Consistency is "how fresh is the read?" (weak → eventual → strong). Availability is "what happens when a box dies?" (fail-over) plus "how do we score uptime?" (nines, and the sequence-vs-parallel math).

Part 1 — Consistency patterns

You write a value. A split second later, someone reads it. Do they get the new value or the old one? The answer is a choice you make when designing a system — and it's a spectrum from "cheap and fast but maybe stale" to "always correct but slower."

Weak consistency

After a write, a read might not see it — best effort, no promises. That sounds broken, but it's perfect when stale-for-a-moment is harmless and speed is everything. A dropped second on a video call is gone and nobody re-fetches it. Memcached behaves this way.

Eventual consistency

After a write, reads will see it — just not instantly. The data spreads to other copies asynchronously, usually within milliseconds. DNS and email work like this: you change a DNS record and it propagates over time. Great when you'd rather stay available than block waiting for everyone to agree.

Strong consistency

After a write, every following read sees it, immediately. The copies update synchronously — the write isn't "done" until everyone agrees. This is your bank balance, file systems, and classic relational transactions.

Django bridge A single Postgres behind your Django app gives you strong consistency for free — you .save(), the next query sees it. The moment you add a read replica to take read load off the primary, you've quietly stepped into eventual consistency: a read from the replica can lag the primary by a few milliseconds (replication lag). That's the exact tradeoff this lesson is about — you didn't change your code, you changed your consistency guarantee.
Watch the words "Synchronous" = wait until copies agree (→ strong). "Asynchronous" = write now, copy later (→ eventual). Same two words show up everywhere in system design — lock them in here.

Part 2 — Availability: fail-over

Availability is about staying up when a machine inevitably dies. The basic trick is to keep a second server around. There are two flavors.

Active–Passive

One server does all the work; a second one sits idle but listening. They exchange a heartbeat — a regular "still alive?" ping. When the heartbeat stops, the passive server grabs the active server's IP address and takes over. How fast it recovers depends on whether the spare is hot (already running) or cold (has to boot up first).

Active–Active

Both servers handle traffic at the same time and split the load. If one dies, the other just absorbs everything. The catch: everyone has to know about both — public-facing means DNS lists both IPs; internal means your app code has to know both exist.

The cost of failover (don't skip this) Failover isn't free: it adds hardware and operational complexity, and there's a real data-loss risk — if the active server dies before it copied the newest writes to the standby, those writes are gone. This is a tradeoff worth naming out loud (remember Lesson 1, step 5).

Part 3 — Availability in numbers (the "nines")

"Highly available" is meaningless until you put a number on it. We measure uptime in nines. More nines = less allowed downtime. The jump from three to four nines is a 10× reduction in acceptable downtime — and usually a big jump in cost.

AvailabilityNameDowntime / yearDowntime / day
99.9%three nines~8h 46m~1m 26s
99.99%four nines~52m 36s~8.6s

Why combining services changes the number

Here's the part that catches people out. When you chain services together, the math is not what your gut says.

The two formulas In sequence (request must pass through both): multiply → availability drops.
A × B. Two 99.9% services in a row = 99.8%.

In parallel (either one can serve it): availability climbs.
1 − (1−A)(1−B). Two 99.9% services side-by-side = 99.9999%.

Plain-English intuition: a chain is only as strong as all its links holding at once, so every extra link drags the total down. A backup only fails if both fail at the same time, which is rare — so redundancy multiplies your nines.

Your win today You can now read the scariest part of the Primer without flinching: consistency is a freshness spectrum you choose (weak→eventual→strong), failover keeps you up with a spare or a twin, and the nines have real math — chains multiply down, backups multiply up. In an interview, saying "those services are in sequence so our effective availability is lower than each one" is a genuine senior signal.
