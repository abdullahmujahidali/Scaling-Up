System Design · Lesson 01

The Framework: how to never freeze in a design interview

One repeatable scaffold for every "Design X" question — and every architecture conversation at work.

~10 min Skill: structure a design from scratch Win: a 5-step script you can run on autopilot

Here's the thing that trips people up: a question like "Design Twitter" isn't really a knowledge test. It's a communication test. The interviewer wants to see if you can take something huge and vague and break it down calmly, in order, out loud. The same is true in a design review at work — the engineer who looks senior is usually just the one with a structure.

Good news: that structure is learnable, and it's the same five steps every time. Learn this once and you have a script to fall back on when your brain goes blank.

The whole lesson in one line Every design follows: Scope → Estimate → Draw → Deep-dive → Trade off. Run them in order, narrate as you go.

The five steps

1 · Scope — pin down what and how big

Don't touch architecture yet. First ask questions to shrink the problem. "Design Twitter" could mean a hundred things — so you decide which slice you're building. Nail down two things: the functional requirements (what features? e.g. "post a tweet, see a feed" — and explicitly cut the rest) and the non-functional requirements (how many users, read-heavy or write-heavy, how fast must it feel, can it ever show slightly stale data?).

Django bridge You already do step 1 whenever you start a Django app: before writing models you ask "what are the core entities and the main user actions?" System design just adds the scale questions on top — how many of these requests per second, and how much data.

2 · Estimate — do the napkin math

A few rough numbers change every later decision. If it's 100 requests/sec, one server is fine. If it's 1,000,000 requests/sec, you're now talking about many servers, caching, and sharding. You don't need exact figures — you need the right order of magnitude. (We'll drill this properly in a later lesson; for now just know it's a required step, not optional.)

3 · Draw — boxes and arrows

Sketch the happy path at a high level: client → load balancer → app servers → database, plus a cache and maybe a queue. Keep it simple and complete first. This drawing becomes the map you'll point at for the rest of the interview.

4 · Deep-dive — go deep on 1–2 hard parts

This is where the interview is actually won or lost. The interviewer picks (or you offer) the spicy part — "how does the feed get built?", "how do you keep this consistent?" — and you go deep on just that. Breadth in step 3, depth here.

5 · Trade off — name what you gave up

Every choice costs something. Senior engineers say the cost out loud: "I'm using a cache here for speed, but that means the data can be a few seconds stale — for a feed that's fine." Naming the tradeoff is the single clearest signal of seniority, in interviews and in real design reviews.

The #1 mistake Jumping straight to step 3 (drawing databases) without doing step 1. You'll build the wrong thing impressively. Always scope first — even one clarifying question buys you credibility and thinking time.

Lock it in — active recall

Don't just nod. Cover the answer, say the five steps out loud, then reveal. Retrieving from memory is what makes it stick.

Tradeoff drill

This is the muscle you'll use most. Read the scenario, pick the move you'd defend, get instant feedback. There's a "better" answer, but the reasoning matters more than the pick.

Your win today You have a 5-step script — Scope, Estimate, Draw, Deep-dive, Trade off — that works on any "Design X" question and any architecture discussion. Next time someone throws a vague systems question at you, you're not staring at a blank page. You're running step 1.
